## What is CCL?

The **Commercial Control License (CCL)** is a source-available software license designed to give the author full control over how their software is used commercially, while still allowing free private evaluation and non-commercial use.

CCL is **not** an open-source license in the OSI sense. It does not grant blanket freedom to use the software in any product or service. Instead, it separates use cases into two lanes:

- **Non-commercial use** → free, no approval needed (within AUM boundaries)
- **Commercial use** → requires explicit written permission from the author via a signed Commercial Authorization Agreement (CAA)

This model is designed for developers and creators who want their work to be accessible for learning, evaluation, and community use — but who want to retain economic and strategic control over commercial exploitation of their software.

The binding written contract, mutually executed and signed by both the Author and the User, that defines the specific terms, scope, and conditions of any permitted Commercial Use. Without a valid CAA, no commercial use is authorized under any circumstances.

1. The User has contacted the Author and obtained explicit written Permission, formalized in a **Commercial Authorization Agreement (CAA)**.

### What does the CAA cover?

The CAA is a flexible instrument. Depending on what is negotiated, it may define:

- The specific scope of commercial use permitted (e.g., only within a specific product, market, or geography)
- Distribution rights — whether the commercial product may be distributed openly or must remain closed-source
- Duration of the agreement
- Payment terms, royalties, or licensing fees (if applicable)
- Software support obligations
- Liability disclaimers specific to the commercial context

**If the CAA does not address a particular topic (e.g., distribution), the default restriction applies — the Software may not be distributed.**

### Making claims under a CAA

Both parties are limited strictly to claims that are **explicitly stated** in the CAA. Any claim outside the scope of the CAA is invalid and can be refused by either party. This protects both the Author and the User from scope creep and legal ambiguity.

Ambiguous cases (e.g., a free app that may later monetize, or a blog that displays ads) should be treated with caution. If there is any doubt about whether your use is commercial, contact the Author before proceeding.

## Commercial Authorization Agreement (CAA)

The CAA is the legal instrument that unlocks commercial use of this Software. It is a binding, written contract between the Author and the User.

### Key properties of the CAA:

- It must be **mutually signed** by both parties.
- It **does not replace or override** the CCL — it operates on top of it.
- Claims enforceable under a CAA are **limited to what is explicitly written** in the CAA.
- If the CAA is silent on distribution and sharing, the Software **cannot** be distributed commercially.
- If the CAA is ambiguous about whether AUM activities will receive Software Support, the default is that **AUM is not supported**.

### To initiate a CAA:

Contact the Author (see [How to Request Permission](#how-to-request-permission)) with:
- A description of the Software you intend to use
- Your intended commercial use case
- The scope, duration, and distribution intentions of your project

## FAQ

**Q: Can I use this Software in my open-source project?**
Yes, provided the project is genuinely non-commercial and you preserve the CCL license text. If your open-source project is sponsored, monetized through ads, or used as a vehicle for paid services, this may qualify as commercial use — contact the Author to clarify.

**Q: Can I fork the Software and publish the fork publicly?**
Yes, for non-commercial purposes. The fork must retain the CCL license text and must not be distributed commercially without a CAA.

**Q: My company is evaluating this Software for potential purchase. Is that okay?**
Evaluation is covered by AUM. You may run and test the Software privately for this purpose. You may not deploy it in production or integrate it into a commercial product without a CAA.

**Q: What happens if I accidentally violate the license?**
All rights are automatically terminated. The fact that the violation was accidental does not prevent the termination. You must stop all use immediately and contact the Author to seek a new Permission or CAA if you wish to continue.

**Q: Does the CCL allow me to sublicense the Software?**
No. Sublicensing is a form of commercial distribution and requires explicit authorization in a CAA.

**Q: What if the Author disappears or becomes unreachable?**
CCL v0.1 does not include a provision for this scenario. Without the Author's Permission, commercial use remains prohibited regardless of reachability. This is a known limitation of the current version.

**Q: Is a verbal agreement with the Author valid as Permission?**
No. Permission must be formally signed or provided in writing via email. Verbal agreements are not valid under CCL.

**Q: Can the Author change the license terms later?**
Yes. The Author can release future versions of CCL. However, Software distributed under CCL v0.1 remains governed by CCL v0.1 unless a new agreement is explicitly made.
